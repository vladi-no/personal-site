Your digital home — that doesn’t sell your data.

Email, maps, storage, identity, notes, calendars, messaging, mobility: these are no longer “apps”. They’re everyday utilities — and too many are funded by surveillance, advertising, or monopoly pricing.

The goal: build a suite of open, transparent, high-quality utilities people genuinely prefer — and make ad-driven software quietly unnecessary.

What success looks like

People choose this ecosystem because it’s better — not because they’re willing to “settle” for ethics. The experience should feel cohesive, calm, and premium. No dark patterns. No attention traps.

Non‑negotiables

  • No selling personal data — ever.
  • No behavioral advertising or tracking.
  • No monopoly price hikes “because we can”.
  • Exit is always possible: export your data, migrate providers, self-host.

Why this exists

Utilities got captured by ad incentives

When revenue depends on attention and profiling, products optimize for engagement and consumption — not for people. This is bad for democracy, human rights, and sustainability.

Design principle: revenue should come from users (membership), not from exploiting users.

Centralized platforms are political choke points

When a few companies control identity, email, storage, and maps, they become global gatekeepers. They will comply with whoever holds authority in their jurisdiction.

Resilience comes from: federation, decentralized hosting, jurisdictional diversity, and cryptographic guarantees.

Nuance: open source alone does not guarantee democratic power. Power lives in governance, funding, and operating control — so those must be designed intentionally.

The model

Funding

  • Membership / subscriptions (primary): predictable, aligned incentives.
  • Donations (optional): for people who want to support the mission further.
  • Institutional sponsorships (optional): universities, NGOs, municipalities.

Goal: cover operating costs, infrastructure, and fair salaries — without ads and without compromising user rights.

Cost-based pricing policy

  • Pricing changes are driven by actual costs, not “what the market will tolerate”.
  • Any change includes a short, readable public explanation.
  • Prices can go down when costs fall or efficiency improves.
  • Premium tiers exist for extra storage/compute — not for basic dignity.
Principle: users should never fear being trapped and then price-gouged.

Governance & trust

Institution design

This is not just software. It’s an institution users can trust. Governance should be explicit, legible, and hard to capture.

  • Clear bylaws and public decision-making process.
  • Roadmap transparency: what is decided by maintainers vs. community input.
  • Conflict-of-interest rules (sponsors don’t buy product direction).
  • Fork-friendly posture (the community always has an escape hatch).

Transparency dashboard (public)

Make the “how” visible, not just the “what”.

  • Infra spend: hosting, bandwidth, storage, third-party services.
  • Team spend: aggregated salary bands (not personal salaries).
  • Runway: cash on hand and months of operation at current burn.
  • Usage metrics that respect privacy (aggregate-only).
The dashboard is accountability and community education: it teaches what it costs to run digital utilities responsibly.

Architecture: federation, portability, resilience

Federation-first design

  • Multiple providers can run compatible nodes of each service.
  • Users can migrate between providers without losing identity.
  • Interoperability is defined by open protocols, not private APIs.
  • Default experience remains simple: federation is available, not mandatory.

Jurisdictional diversity

  • Encourage nodes in multiple legal jurisdictions.
  • Enable communities to host their own infrastructure.
  • Minimize “global chokepoints” (single CDN, payment provider, registrar).

Cryptographic guarantees

Use cryptography to make “trust” less dependent on any single operator. Not maximum cryptography everywhere — right-sized cryptography that protects user rights without breaking usability.

Data portability

  • One-click export of your data in standard formats.
  • Backups that don’t require trusting a single vendor.
  • Migration paths between providers.

Security posture

  • Threat modeling as part of product design.
  • Secure-by-default configs and updates.
  • Independent audits when feasible.
  • Responsible disclosure process.

Product strategy

Start narrow. Make it exceptional. Expand gradually.

The first product is the credibility engine. It must be so good that people say: “This is awesome — I’m never going back.”

Reuse existing open-source foundations wherever possible (e.g., OpenStreetMap for mapping, established storage stacks, proven protocols). Don’t duplicate plumbing. Differentiate through integration, UX, and trust.
PhaseWhat gets builtWhy it matters
Phase 0 Foundations Identity, billing/membership, transparency dashboard, shared design system, export/migration tooling. Build the spine: cohesion, trust, and portability. Make future apps cheaper to add.
Phase 1 First utility One “daily use” product with premium UX and strong privacy guarantees (e.g., personal “digital home”: files + notes + sync). Prove the model. Win trust. Get to meaningful paying users.
Phase 2 Adjacent utilities Add next modules that share identity/storage (calendar, mail, photos, contacts), keeping migration and federation intact. Cohesion increases value. Trust compounds across products.
Phase 3 Network‑effect apps Apps requiring local density and stronger safety systems (e.g., ride-sharing / hitchhiking). Do these once reputation, governance, and operational maturity exist.
Rule of thumb: foundations first, then globally scalable utilities, then network-effect products.

UX philosophy

Opinionated defaults

  • 90% of users should never need a settings screen.
  • “It just works” is part of the mission — not a luxury.
  • Performance is a feature: fast, calm, predictable.

No dark patterns

  • No infinite scroll traps or engagement hooks.
  • No manipulative prompts or “growth hacks”.
  • Privacy settings are not buried.

Design system

A shared design system ensures consistency across modules: typography, spacing, interaction patterns, accessibility, and calm visual language.

Aesthetic: trustworthy, human, durable. No aggressive sales vibe.

Ethical “advertising” (optional, later)

If “advertising” exists at all, it should be designed like a public noticeboard — not a surveillance machine. Think: informing people, not manipulating them.

  • Contextual only (no tracking, no profiling).
  • Opt‑in and clearly labeled.
  • Transparent rules and public revenue breakdown.
  • No psychological optimization, no engagement-driven bidding.
Default funding remains membership. Ethical “ads” are secondary — only if they don’t corrupt incentives.

AI as leverage (not a crutch)

Where AI helps

  • Boilerplate, refactors, tests.
  • Infra automation and configuration.
  • Rapid prototyping for UX exploration.
  • Docs, onboarding copy, internal tooling.

Where AI doesn’t replace work

  • Product taste and coherent UX decisions.
  • Security judgment and threat modeling.
  • Governance, trust, community building.
  • Long-term maintenance and operations.
Principle: use AI to reduce friction, then spend saved time on quality, security, and the human side of trust.

Risks & mitigations

Key risks

Scope creep — too many utilities too early.
Governance conflict — ideology without process.
Funding volatility — donations alone don’t sustain teams.
Network effects — some products require local density & safety.

Mitigations

Start narrow with a globally scalable utility; ship quality first.
Governance early: bylaws, roles, boundaries, transparency.
Membership-first revenue with optional donations; publish costs.
Defer network-effect apps until maturity.

How to start (pragmatic plan)

Launch strategy

  1. Pick the first utility based on subscription viability, global scalability, low liability, and “spine” value.
  2. Ship hosted-by-default (zero setup) plus a clear self-hosting path.
  3. Build the trust layer: dashboard, export/migration, public roadmap & governance.
  4. Obsess over UX: onboarding, performance, reliability, calm design.
  5. Earn the first 1,000 paying users with a product they prefer, not just support ideologically.
After the first product proves the model, every next utility is cheaper: shared identity, shared design, shared billing, shared trust.

What we measure (without tracking people)

  • Retention & satisfaction (privacy-preserving).
  • Reliability (uptime, incident response).
  • Cost efficiency and price stability.
  • Migration success (export/move/self-host).
  • Community health (contributors, review throughput).

What we refuse to measure

  • Engagement for engagement’s sake.
  • Manipulative funnel metrics.
  • Behavioral profiles.
  • Anything requiring surveillance to compute.
Principle: metrics should serve quality and sustainability — not exploitation.

Cabin Fever

A face-up card + dice game with a growing pot

Players
2–6 (5–6 ideal)
What you need
1–2 decks • 2 dice • coins
Goal
Be first to flip all your cards face-down

1) Components

  • Playing cards: 2 standard 52-card decks (or 1 deck for smaller groups — see Setup)
  • Two six-sided dice
  • Coins for the pot (nickels, dimes, etc.)

2) Setup

  1. Deck count: Use two decks for 5–6 players. For fewer than 5 players, use one deck.
  2. Coin buy-in (typical): Before the game starts, the group agrees on a starting amount of coins. Everyone starts with the same amount.
  3. Elimination (typical): If a player runs out of coins at any time, they are out of the game.
  4. Optional mode: You can also play with infinite coins so nobody can be eliminated for running out.
  5. Combine the deck(s) you are using into one stack.
  6. Remove all Kings, all Sevens, and all Aces.
  7. Shuffle the remaining cards well.
  8. Deal all remaining cards as evenly as possible among players.
  9. Each player lays their cards face-up in front of them. Everyone is allowed to see everyone’s cards at all times.
  10. Put a space/pile in the middle for the pot.
  11. Starting pot ante: Before the game begins, every player puts one dime (10¢) into the pot.

Card Values Used in This Game

During play, number cards represent their face value. Face cards have special meaning: Jack = 11 and Queen = 12 for matching dice rolls.

For end-of-game scoring only, Jacks and Queens are worth 10¢ each.

3) Starting Player

Each player looks only at their own face-up cards and identifies the best poker hand they can make from those cards (typical poker ranking).

The player with the best poker hand starts the game. If there’s a tie, the tied players can pick a simple tiebreaker (e.g., compare highest card) or just agree on who goes first.

Before rolling the dice for the first time, the starting player takes all the dimes from the pot.

4) How Turns Work

On your turn:

  1. Roll the two dice and add them together.
  2. If you can “cover” the roll, you flip card(s) face-down and keep rolling.
  3. If you cannot cover the roll, your turn ends (see the next section).

Face cards as natural matches

During play, Jacks are treated as 11 and Queens are treated as 12 when matching dice rolls.

This applies anywhere a natural match is allowed, including when other players flip cards after someone cannot cover a roll.

4.1 Natural match

If the total you rolled matches a card you have exactly (example: roll 5 and you have a 5; roll 11 and you have a Jack; roll 12 and you have a Queen), that is a natural.

Flip that matching card face-down.

4.2 Two-card combination

If you do not have the natural card for the roll, you may flip a maximum of two cards that add up to the rolled total.

  • Example: roll 8 → flip 5 + 3 (two cards).
  • You may not use three (or more) cards to make a total.

Important

On your own turn, you may cover a roll either with a natural (one card) or with a two-card combo—as long as you have the cards available.

5) When You Can’t Match a Roll

If you roll a total and you have no natural for that number and no valid two-card combination that equals that number, then:

  1. Your turn ends immediately.
  2. Any other player(s) who have the natural card for the number you rolled may flip that card face-down.

Natural-only rule on “steal flips”

When other players flip cards because you couldn’t cover a roll, they may flip only the natural single card (including Jack for 11 and Queen for 12).
They may not use combinations in this situation.

6) The Seven Rule (Pot Building)

Even though all 7 cards were removed from the deck, rolling a 7 on the dice triggers payments into the pot.

Whenever you roll a 7 on your turn:

  • All other players (everyone except the roller) pay into the pot.
  • The amount depends on how many 7s you have rolled during this same turn.
  • Each additional 7 rolled during the same turn increases the amount by 5¢.
# of 7s rolled this turn Each other player pays
1st 7 5¢
2nd 7 10¢
3rd 7 15¢
4th 7 20¢

Not consecutive

The 7s do not need to be rolled back-to-back. Example: If you roll 7 (5¢), then later roll 8 and flip it, then later roll 7 again, the second 7 still increases to 10¢ (because it’s the 2nd seven of that turn).

7) Ending & Payout

7.1 How the game ends

The game ends immediately when a player flips all their cards face-down.

There is no “fewest cards” ending condition. Someone must fully clear their layout to win.

7.2 Winner collects the pot

The winner takes all the money that accumulated in the center pot from the Seven Rule and final payments.

7.3 Final payments based on remaining cards

After the winner clears all their cards, every other player totals the value of the cards they still have face-up:

  • Jack = 10¢ (end-of-game scoring only)
  • Queen = 10¢ (end-of-game scoring only)
  • Number cards = their face value (10=10¢, 9=9¢, 8=8¢, etc.)

Add the values, then round down to the nearest 5¢ (nearest nickel). Pay that many cents to the winner.

Example

If your remaining cards total 42, round down to 40 → you pay 40¢.

8) Examples

Example A: Natural vs combo

You roll a total of 8.

  • If you have an 8 card, flip the 8 (natural).
  • If you don’t have an 8, but you have 5 and 3, flip both (combo).
  • You may not use 2+2+4 (three cards). Max is two cards.

Example B: “Can’t match” ends turn + other players flip naturals

You roll 8 but you have no 8 card and no two-card combo that makes 8. Your turn ends immediately.

Any other player(s) who have a natural 8 may flip their 8 face-down. They may not flip 5+3 for this bonus—only the natural 8.

Example C: Seven escalation during a turn

During your turn you roll:

  1. 7 → every other player pays 5¢
  2. 8 (you can cover it) → you flip your card(s) and keep rolling
  3. 7 again → every other player now pays 10¢

The 7s didn’t have to be consecutive. The second 7 still increases the payment.

9) Notes & Clarifications

  • This ruleset assumes you continue rolling on your turn as long as you successfully flip card(s) for each roll.
  • Because 7s are removed from the deck, rolling 7 is purely a pot mechanic.
  • Coin rules can be played either as elimination (typical) or infinite coins (no elimination). Choose as a group before starting.
  • The starting player takes the initial dime antes before the first roll; after that, the pot is primarily built by the Seven Rule and final payments.
